In the Kohn–Sham framework, all the complexity of many-body quantum mechanics is concentrated in
a single term: the **exchange-correlation (XC) energy** $E_{\rm xc}[\rho]$. Its functional
derivative, $V_{\rm xc}(\mathbf{r}) = \delta E_{\rm xc}/\delta\rho(\mathbf{r})$, enters the
effective potential and shapes every KS orbital. If the exact $E_{\rm xc}$ were known, DFT would
be an exact theory. In practice, $E_{\rm xc}$ must be approximated, and the quality of that
approximation determines the quality of the calculation.

Over six decades, a hierarchy of approximations has been developed — colloquially organised as
**Jacob's Ladder** (Perdew, 2001), ascending from the crude but fast to the accurate but
expensive. This chapter surveys each rung: its formal construction, physical content, known
strengths and weaknesses, and practical guidance on when to use it.

---

## Formal Definition

The XC energy is defined by the decomposition of the true energy functional introduced in
Chapter 3:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\rm xc}[\rho] = \underbrace{(T[\rho] - T_s[\rho])}_{\text{kinetic correlation}}
    + \underbrace{(V_{ee}[\rho] - E_{\rm H}[\rho])}_{\text{exchange + Coulomb correlation}}.
\end{equation}

It can also be expressed exactly via the **adiabatic connection formula**:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\rm xc}[\rho] = \int_0^1 \langle\Psi^\lambda|\hat{V}_{ee}|\Psi^\lambda\rangle\,d\lambda
    - E_{\rm H}[\rho],
\end{equation}
where $\Psi^\lambda$ is the ground-state wavefunction of a system with the same density
$\rho(\mathbf{r})$ but with the electron–electron interaction scaled by $\lambda \in [0,1]$.
At $\lambda = 0$ this is the non-interacting KS system; at $\lambda = 1$ it is the fully
interacting physical system. This formula provides both a rigorous definition and a conceptual
link to the hybrid functional construction below.

## Jacob's Ladder of DFT Approximations

Jacob's Ladder organises XC approximations by the ingredients they use to construct
$E_{\rm xc}$ at each point in space:

| Rung | Name | Ingredients | Example functionals |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | LDA | $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ | SVWN, PW92 |
| 2 | GGA | $\rho$, $\nabla\rho$ | PBE, BLYP, PW91 |
| 3 | meta-GGA | $\rho$, $\nabla\rho$, $\tau$ | SCAN, TPSS, r$^2$SCAN |
| 4 | Hybrid | + fraction of exact exchange | B3LYP, PBE0, HSE06 |
| 5 | Double-hybrid | + perturbation theory correlation | B2PLYP, PBE-QIDH |
| — | (heaven) | Exact $E_{\rm xc}$ | — |

### Rung 1: Local Density Approximation (LDA)

The LDA, proposed in the original Kohn–Sham paper (1965), is the simplest possible
approximation: at each point $\mathbf{r}$, use the XC energy density of a **uniform electron gas
(UEG)** at the local density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\rm xc}^{\rm LDA}[\rho] = \int \varepsilon_{\rm xc}^{\rm UEG}(\rho(\mathbf{r}))\,\rho(\mathbf{r})\,d\mathbf{r}.
\end{equation}

The exchange part is known analytically (Dirac, 1930):
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_{\rm x}^{\rm UEG}(\rho) = -\frac{3}{4}\left(\frac{3}{\pi}\right)^{1/3}\rho^{1/3},
\end{equation}
while the correlation part $\varepsilon_{\rm c}$ is obtained by fitting to quantum Monte Carlo
(QMC) data for the UEG (Ceperley and Alder, 1980). Common parameterisations include
Perdew–Wang (PW92) and Vosko–Wilk–Nusair (VWN).

**Why LDA works better than expected.** For slowly varying densities, the LDA is justified by
a gradient expansion. But even for inhomogeneous systems like atoms and molecules, LDA often
performs surprisingly well because of a **systematic error cancellation**: LDA overestimates
exchange and underestimates correlation in ways that partially cancel. Moreover, the LDA XC hole
(the depletion of electron density around each electron) satisfies the exact sum rule
$\int n_{\rm xc}(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r}')\,d\mathbf{r}' = -1$ by construction, a key constraint
that is violated by some higher-rung approximations.

**Known errors:**
- Overbinds bonds (cohesive energies overestimated by $\sim 1$–$2$ eV/atom).
- Underestimates lattice constants (by $\sim 1$–$3\%$) and band gaps (systematically).
- Fails for strongly correlated systems and van der Waals interactions.

**When to use LDA:** Phonon calculations in metals, where error cancellation is effective and
gradient corrections offer little improvement; structural studies of simple metals; as a baseline.

---

### Rung 2: Generalised Gradient Approximation (GGA)

GGAs improve on LDA by including dependence on the local density **gradient**
$\nabla\rho(\mathbf{r})$, correcting for density inhomogeneity:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\rm xc}^{\rm GGA}[\rho] = \int \varepsilon_{\rm xc}^{\rm GGA}\!\left(\rho(\mathbf{r}),\,|\nabla\rho(\mathbf{r})|\right)\rho(\mathbf{r})\,d\mathbf{r}.
\end{equation}

The most widely used GGA functional is **PBE** (Perdew, Burke, Ernzerhof, 1996). Unlike
empirically fitted predecessors, PBE is derived from first principles by satisfying a set of
exact constraints on $E_{\rm xc}$ (sum rules, scaling relations, correct large-gradient limit).
This constraint-based philosophy makes PBE transferable across chemistry and materials science.

**Comparison with LDA:**

| Property | LDA error | PBE error |
|---|---|---|
| Bond lengths / lattice constants | $-1$ to $-3\%$ (underestimates) | $+1$ to $+2\%$ (overestimates) |
| Atomisation energies | Overestimates ($\sim 30$ kcal/mol) | Overestimates ($\sim 10$ kcal/mol) |
| Band gaps | Underestimates by $\sim 40\%$ | Underestimates by $\sim 40\%$ |
| Hydrogen bonds | Reasonable | Good |
| Van der Waals | Very poor | Poor (no non-local correlation) |

**Common GGAs:**
- **PBE**: Default for periodic solids and surfaces; excellent transferability.
- **BLYP** (Becke exchange + Lee–Yang–Parr correlation): Popular in quantum chemistry.
- **PBEsol**: Re-parametrised PBE for better performance on solids and surfaces (restores gradient expansion).
- **RPBE**: Improved chemisorption energies on transition metal surfaces.

**When to use GGA/PBE:** Geometry optimisations of molecules and solids; equation-of-state
calculations; surface and catalysis studies. The default choice for most materials calculations.

### Rung 3: Meta-GGA

Meta-GGAs add a third local ingredient: the **kinetic energy density** of the KS orbitals,
\begin{equation}
    \tau(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^N |\nabla\phi_i(\mathbf{r})|^2,
\end{equation}
which provides information about the local chemical environment (e.g. whether the region is
single-bond, metallic, or van der Waals in character) that $\rho$ and $\nabla\rho$ alone cannot
distinguish:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\rm xc}^{\rm meta\text{-}GGA}[\rho] = \int \varepsilon_{\rm xc}\!\left(\rho,\,|\nabla\rho|,\,\tau\right)\rho\,d\mathbf{r}.
\end{equation}

The **iso-orbital indicator**
\begin{equation}
    \alpha(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\tau(\mathbf{r}) - \tau^W(\mathbf{r})}{\tau^{\rm UEG}(\mathbf{r})},
    \qquad \tau^W = \frac{|\nabla\rho|^2}{8\rho},
\end{equation}
distinguishes: $\alpha \approx 0$ (single-bond, covalent), $\alpha \approx 1$ (metallic), and
$\alpha \gg 1$ (weak interaction, van der Waals) regions. This enables the functional to behave
differently in chemically distinct environments.

**Key functionals:**
- **SCAN** (Strongly Constrained and Appropriately Normed, Sun et al. 2015): Satisfies all 17
  known exact constraints applicable to a semi-local functional. Generally the best meta-GGA,
  with improved lattice constants, band gaps, and surface energies over PBE at comparable cost.
- **r²SCAN**: Numerically regularised version of SCAN that avoids convergence problems in
  plane-wave codes; preferred in practice.
- **TPSS**: Earlier meta-GGA, less accurate than SCAN but historically important.

**When to use meta-GGA:** When GGA accuracy is insufficient but hybrid cost is prohibitive (e.g.
large supercells, many-atom simulations); magnetic systems; strongly hydrogen-bonded systems;
accurate lattice constants for transition metal oxides.

### Rung 4: Hybrid Functionals

The adiabatic connection formula motivates a conceptually clean improvement: mix a fraction $a$
of **exact (Hartree–Fock) exchange** with semi-local exchange:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\rm xc}^{\rm hybrid} = a\,E_{\rm x}^{\rm exact} + (1-a)\,E_{\rm x}^{\rm GGA} + E_{\rm c}^{\rm GGA},
\end{equation}
where the exact exchange is:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\rm x}^{\rm exact} = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}\iint \frac{\phi_i^*(\mathbf{r})\phi_j(\mathbf{r})\phi_j^*(\mathbf{r}')\phi_i(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}\,d\mathbf{r}\,d\mathbf{r}'.
\end{equation}

This non-local term is computationally expensive (scaling formally as $\mathcal{O}(N^4)$ for
molecules, $\mathcal{O}(N^3)$ with screening) but dramatically improves band gaps and reaction
energies.

**Key hybrid functionals:**

- **B3LYP** ($a = 0.20$, with three empirical parameters): The most cited functional in
  chemistry; excellent for molecular geometries, thermochemistry ($\sim 3$ kcal/mol), and organic
  molecules. Poor for solids and metals.

- **PBE0** ($a = 0.25$, derived from perturbation theory): Better transferability than B3LYP;
  good for both molecules and solids. Band gaps improved but still underestimated by $\sim
  20\%$.

- **HSE06** (Heyd–Scuseria–Ernzerhof): Screened hybrid — exact exchange is applied only at
  **short range** ($r < r_{\rm screen} \approx 10$ Å), with GGA exchange at long range:
  \begin{equation}
      E_{\rm x}^{\rm HSE} = a\,E_{\rm x}^{\rm exact,SR}(\omega) + (1-a)\,E_{\rm x}^{\rm PBE,SR}(\omega) + E_{\rm x}^{\rm PBE,LR}(\omega),
  \end{equation}
  where $\omega$ is the screening parameter. This avoids the divergence of exact exchange in
  metals and makes HSE06 tractable for periodic solids. It is the standard choice for
  semiconductor band gaps.

**When to use hybrid functionals:** Band gap calculations; magnetic exchange coupling
constants $J$; reaction barrier heights; optical properties (qualitative). Use PBE0 or HSE06 for
solids, B3LYP for organic molecules. Avoid for metals (unphysical behaviour of long-range
exact exchange).

---

### Rung 5: Double-Hybrid Functionals

Double hybrids add a fraction of **second-order Møller–Plesset (MP2) correlation** to the
hybrid framework:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\rm xc}^{\rm DH} = a\,E_{\rm x}^{\rm exact} + (1-a)\,E_{\rm x}^{\rm GGA} + b\,E_{\rm c}^{\rm MP2} + (1-b)\,E_{\rm c}^{\rm GGA}.
\end{equation}

MP2 correlation captures medium-range electron correlation and dispersion, improving
thermochemistry to $\sim 1$ kcal/mol. The computational cost scales as $\mathcal{O}(N^5)$,
restricting use to systems of tens to a few hundred atoms.

**Key functionals:** B2PLYP, PBE-QIDH, $\omega$B97X-2.

**When to use:** High-accuracy thermochemistry; non-covalent interactions; benchmark studies.
Rarely used for periodic solids due to cost.

## Van der Waals Corrections

A systematic failure of semi-local functionals (LDA, GGA, meta-GGA) is the inability to capture
**London dispersion** (van der Waals) interactions, which arise from long-range correlation
between fluctuating multipoles. These functionals decay too quickly at large separations,
leading to poor descriptions of layered materials (graphite, MoS$_2$), molecular crystals, and
adsorption on surfaces.

The most practical remedies are **dispersion corrections** appended to standard functionals:

- **DFT-D3** (Grimme, 2010): Add an atom-pairwise $-C_6/r^6$ term with a damping function
  at short range. Simple, cheap, and widely available. The BJ damping variant (DFT-D3(BJ)) is
  recommended.
- **Tkatchenko–Scheffler (TS)**: $C_6$ coefficients depend on the local chemical environment
  via the Hirshfeld partitioning of $\rho$.
- **Many-body dispersion (MBD)**: Captures collective (many-body) dispersion, important for
  large molecules and molecular crystals.
- **vdW-DF family** (Dion et al.): Non-local correlation functional that includes dispersion at
  the functional level.

For most purposes, **PBE-D3(BJ)** or **PBE+MBD** offers a good balance of accuracy and cost for
periodic systems.

## Practical Decision Guide

The following table summarises recommended choices by property and system type:

| Property / System | Recommended functional | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Lattice constants, phonons (metals) | LDA or PBEsol | Error cancellation favourable |
| Geometry optimisation (general) | PBE or SCAN | PBE default; SCAN for better quality |
| Band gaps (semiconductors) | HSE06 or PBE0 | Screened hybrid essential |
| Magnetic exchange coupling $J$ | PBE0 or HSE06 | Exact exchange corrects self-interaction |
| Molecular thermochemistry | B3LYP or PBE0 | Double-hybrid if high accuracy needed |
| Layered / van der Waals systems | PBE-D3(BJ) or MBD | Dispersion correction mandatory |
| Large supercells ($> 200$ atoms) | PBE or r²SCAN | Hybrid cost prohibitive |
| Reaction barriers | Hybrid or double-hybrid | Semi-local underestimates barriers |

## Self-Interaction Error

A fundamental pathology of approximate XC functionals is the **self-interaction error (SIE)**.
In the exact theory, the Hartree energy $E_{\rm H}[\rho]$ includes the spurious
interaction of each electron with itself; this must be exactly cancelled by the corresponding
term in $E_{\rm xc}$. For the exact functional, this cancellation is complete. For approximate
functionals, it is not, leading to errors that are especially severe for:

- Stretched bonds and transition states (delocalization error).
- Strongly correlated systems ($d$- and $f$-electron compounds).
- Charge transfer excitations and negative ions.

Hybrid functionals reduce the SIE by incorporating exact exchange (which is self-interaction
free by construction), but do not eliminate it. The **DFT+U** method (Chapter 6) provides a
targeted correction for localised $d$ and $f$ orbitals.

## Outlook

Despite decades of development, no universally accurate approximate XC functional exists. The
choice of functional remains one of the most consequential — and contested — decisions in a DFT
study. The next chapter addresses a prerequisite of equal practical importance: how to represent
the KS orbitals numerically using basis sets and pseudopotentials.
